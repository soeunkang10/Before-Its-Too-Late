Why I made this

"I started by asking one question at a pharmacy counter: 'Do you carry Narcan?' I didn't have a plan. I just wanted to know the answer. Three years later, that question became a district-level policy."

This toolkit is everything I wish someone had handed me when I started in my advocacy journey. Every step is something I actually did in my community, which ranged from doing small-level awareness campaigns in local community centers to changing policies with the approval of my school board. If you follow this roadmap, or even take away some aspects of it and incorporate it into your own work, you'll leave with more than just awareness. You'll leave ready and prepared to change something.

The Roadmap

7 Steps: From Your First Question
to Real Policy Change

1
Start Here

Map Naloxone Access in Your Community

Before you can change anything, you need to know what's actually out there. Visit pharmacies, hospitals, libraries, and public spaces. Document whether naloxone is available, where it's kept, how much it costs, and whether there's any signage. Then put it all on a map.

What to do
  • Visit at least 10 pharmacies. Note availability, price, location in store, and signage
  • Check hospitals, libraries, and community centers. Look near AED kits installed on walls!
  • Create a Google My Map with color-coded pins (available = white icons/ unavailable = red icons)
  • Document price differences: gaps in pricing are critical advocacy data
How to build your map on Google My Maps
  • Go to Google My Maps and create a new map
  • Search up a location or address
  • Click on the result you're looking for, then press the green pin icon on the map
  • Click "Add to map." Then it will be added to your "layer," which you can rename by pressing on "Untitled Layer"
  • Press the pencil icon on a pin (which will now appear blue) to add more detailed info (price, signage, notes). Make sure to save!
  • Keep adding locations to the same layer, or press "Add layer" to start a new category of location (e.g., CVS pharmacies vs. Walgreens pharmacies)
💬
My noteI visited 80+ pharmacies across Cleveland and Boston. Prices ranged from $44.99 to $130 for the exact same medication, and in some places, it was hidden or locked away. I didn't expect that. That surprise became the data that started my advocacy journey.
2
Analyze

Find the Gaps and Turn Data into a Story

Numbers alone don't move people, but stories do. Look at your map and find the patterns. Which areas have the least access? Where is naloxone hidden? What does the price gap mean for families who need it most? Turn those patterns into a clear, simple narrative.

What to do
  • Calculate: what % of locations had naloxone visible vs. hidden or unavailable?
  • Overlay your map with county overdose data (your health department often has this publicly)
  • Write down 3-5 data observations/trends that are easy to say out loud and hard to forget
  • Make one simple visual, like a chart or map that tells the whole story at a glance
💬
My noteOrganizing your data in a way that is easy to analyze is such a critical factor in advancing your advocacy work.
3
Document & Publish

Write It Down: Build a Record That Lasts

Publishing your findings gives them credibility and reach. Write for your school paper, submit an opinion piece online, or find another way to share your data (for me, it was Regulations.gov). A public record shows people (especially officials) that you're not just concerned but that you're also committed.

What to do
  • Write a news article for your school paper with your survey findings
  • Write a personal opinion piece about why naloxone access matters to youth specifically
  • Build a simple public website (Google Sites or GitHub Pages) with your map, articles, and data
  • Consider submitting a public comment to an FDA or state health docket if you can find the opportunity to do so
💬
My notePublishing allows you to broaden the reach of your efforts, and also introduces you to new platforms where you can expand your work.
4
Build Allies

If You Haven't Yet, Find Prevention Organizations and Connect with Them

You don't have to do this alone, and you'll go further if you don't. Organizations like SAFE Project, CADCA coalitions, and your county health department have resources, networks, and credibility that can amplify everything you're doing. Find them. Show up. Ask how you can help.

What to do
  • Look up SAFE Project's Youth VOICE Council or apply to CADCA's National Youth Council if you can (I highly suggest joining these 2 councils if possible), but if you find other organizations you wish to be involved with, go ahead!
  • Go to one meeting or event before you ask for anything. Just show up and listen
  • Ask if you can volunteer, host an event, or share your research findings
💬
My noteJoining SAFE Project's Youth VOICE Council changed everything. That one connection led me to the Naloxone Accessibility Challenge, which led to the pharmacy surveys, which led to every step after this.
5
Take Direct Action

Host Community Education Events and Measure Your Impact

This is where research becomes advocacy. Host a session at a library, community center, or your school. Educate people on naloxone, Deterra drug disposal pouches, or anything you want to advocate for: (for me it was) where to find it, how to recognize an overdose, how to respond, how to use drug disposal pouches. Use before-and-after surveys to measure how awareness levels changed. That data becomes your proof when you talk to people, such as policymakers.

What to do
  • Partner with a library, community center, or school club to host an educational session
  • Create a pre & post survey on Google Forms or on Google Docs to print it out. Keep each of them short, 4-5 questions
  • Cover (for example): where to find naloxone, how to recognize an overdose, how to use it
  • Contact your county health department/prevention organizations like SAFE Project for pouches to distribute
  • Document your results: attendance, % change in awareness, feedback from participants
💬
My noteBefore my educational sessions on safe drug disposal, 90% of participants had never heard of at-home drug disposal options. After, 85% said they planned to act immediately and use the pouches to dispose of unused/expired medications. When you can say "90% didn't know this existed," policymakers listen differently.
6
Escalate

Present a Formal Proposal to Your School or District

Now you have something most students don't: real data, a public record, community partners, and documented impact. Take that to your principal. Then your school board. Come with specific asks, evidence, and a clear plan. Make it easy for them to say yes.

What to do
  • Define your proposals clearly and specifically (e.g., naloxone next to AED units, annual health class module, expert speaker)
  • Build a slide deck: problem data → your evidence → proposed solution → implementation plan
  • Start with your principal, not the school board (build from smaller wins first)
  • Bring your map, survey results, and published articles as supporting materials
  • Prepare responses to objections around cost, liability, and stigma
💬
My noteI showed my principal my naloxone accessibility map before I said a single word about what I was about to suggest. I let the map, the data, and the articles speak first. By the time I made the ask, he already understood why it mattered.
7
Win. Then Build Forward.

Secure the Policy and Make It Last

A policy win is not the finish line. It's the starting point. Document everything. Share the news. Train whoever comes after you. Report back to your partner organizations. Then look at how you can expand: to the city level, the state level, and beyond. If your proposals couldn't be passed, don't give up entirely. Find other ways in which you can continue to advocate (there are always other solutions, and these steps that worked in my community may not necessarily work in yours)

What to do
  • Write a follow-up article announcing the policy win
  • Share results with any partners who supported you
  • Create a one-page summary another student could use to replicate your model
  • Find someone younger who can carry the work forward when you leave
💬
My noteOn May 11, 2026, Superintendent Hardis approved both proposals on the spot: naloxone kits adjacent to AED units in every school hallway, and naloxone education added to the annual Health curriculum. He also invited me back to train students again next year. Document everything. Even the small moments matter.

You can find more information and resources supportive of solutions towards overcoming mental health challenges, substance misuse and the addiction epidemic on our website at: https://www.safeproject.us/disposal/

1. Question: Why does safe disposal of prescription medications matter?

Nearly 8.6 million Americans 12 years or older reported misusing prescription opioids in 2023. Findings suggest that household prescription availability is associated with increased odds of opioid overdose for others in the household, even if they do not have their own opioid prescription. A leading source for people aged 12 or older who misused prescription drugs are friends or relatives with 42.3 percent reporting that they obtained the pain relievers from a friend or relative in some way (i.e., being given them, buying them, or taking them without asking).

2. Question: Why should I dispose of prescription drugs that I've paid for? What if I need them someday?

Unused and expired prescription medications create a risk to the people who possess them, their loved ones, and their community. All drugs should be safeguarded; an important part of safeguarding is getting rid of them when they are no longer needed.

3. Question: What are the risks of having prescription medications available in my home?

  • Accidental Use
  • Accidental overdose
  • Intentional misuse
  • Health risks – expired drugs may no longer be effective; they can be harmful to the user. Sharing medications can lead to dangerous health consequences due to dosing, mixing with other medications
  • Safety risks – presence of prescription drugs in your home can make your home a target
  • Environmental damage – having a negative impact on water supplies and land if improperly disposed of

4. Question: What should I know about the benefit of at-home prescription drug disposal?

Proper disposal increased from 19% to 92% when patients were provided with an at-home disposal product and education – 2021 study.

5. Question: Where do I get at-home disposal products?

At-home disposal products can be purchased online (Amazon, Walmart, etc…), in some retail stores, and are available at some pharmacies and through community health departments and coalitions. Ohio residents can also request Deterra pouches at no cost through Ohio Rx: ohiorxdisposal.com

6. Question: How do I use the at-home disposal pouch?

Open the pouch, placing unused medications inside. Fill the pouch halfway with warm water and wait for 30 seconds (foaming may occur). Seal pouch tightly, gently shake, and dispose of in the normal trash. DO NOT open the pouch again after you have sealed it. Watch how-to videos here.

7. Question: What types of medications can I dispose of with the at-home disposal product?

Deterra deactivates virtually all organic over the counter and prescription medications, including common pills, patches, liquids, and creams, especially dangerous ones like opioids, stimulants (Adderall, Ritalin), and depressants (Xanax, Valium, Ambien). Commonly prescribed opioids: Codeine, Hydrocodone, Hydromorphone, Meperidine, Methadone, Morphine, Oxycodone, Oxymorphone, Pethidine. Note: Deterra will not absorb biologics, inorganic medications, or leaf marijuana.

8. Question: How many pills/liquids/patches can be disposed of with the pouches?

  • Small Pouch (SP): 15 pills / 2 oz liquid / 2 patches
  • Medium Pouch (MP): 45 pills / 6 oz liquid / 6 patches
  • Large Pouch (LP): 90 pills / 12 oz liquid / 12 patches

More questions? Visit deterrasystem.com/faqs

Step 5 Resource · Data Collection

Safe Drug Disposal Surveys

Use these pre and post surveys at your community education events to measure impact (adapt and change according to what you are advocating for, this is just an example). Collect before and after your session, because the data you gather becomes your proof when talking to policymakers.

Pre-Education Survey

To be completed before receiving the pouch and education

1. Do you currently have unused or expired prescription medications in your home?

2. How do you usually dispose of unused or expired medications?

3. Did you know that many people who misuse prescription drugs obtain them from friends or relatives?

4. How confident do you feel about knowing how to safely dispose of prescription medications?

5. Before today, were you aware that at-home drug disposal products exist?

Post-Education Survey

To be completed after receiving the pouch and education

1. After today, how well do you understand why safe medication disposal matters?

2. Do you understand which types of medications can be disposed of using the pouch?

3. How confident do you now feel about using the drug-disposal pouch correctly?

4. Do you plan to use the disposal pouch you received today?

Step 5 Resource · Health Class Training

Opioid Overdose Response Training

This is the presentation I used to train students on opioid overdose response during Health class. Use it as a template for your own school training session.

Opening Question

If you saw someone lying unresponsive on the ground with blue lips and cold skin, what would you do first?

Opioid Overdose Symptoms

Non-responsive
Slow/stopped breathing
Blue/gray lips and fingernails
Pale/ashen and clammy skin
Limp body
Vomiting/gurgling
Weak or no pulse

If you get a response:

  • Lay them to their side to prevent choking if they vomit
  • Call 911 if anything is concerning: breathing slows down or they become less responsive

If you DON'T get a response:

  • Move the person to a stable, horizontal, face-up position. Tilt head back to create a clear airway.
  • Call 911 (explain the situation and symptoms!)
  • Follow the next steps to use naloxone

How to Use Naloxone

  1. Peel open the first dose of naloxone.
  2. Hold the nasal spray correctly (thumb on bottom, two fingers on nozzle).
  3. Insert the applicator into a nostril.
  4. Press the plunger firmly with your thumb.

DO NOT TEST THE DEVICE BEFORE USING! THERE ARE ONLY 2 SINGLE-DOSES IN ONE BOX.

IF THERE IS NO REACTION WITHIN 2-3 MINUTES:

Administer second dose of naloxone into the other nostril!

TRUE OR FALSE?

Naloxone is dangerous if used on someone who doesn't need it.

FALSE: it has no effect if opioids aren't present.

TRUE OR FALSE?

There is an age requirement to carry naloxone.

FALSE: no minimum age requirement.

TRUE OR FALSE?

Naloxone is available at our school.

Update this answer for your own school!

Step 6 Resource · Policy Proposal Presentation

Strengthening Opioid Overdose Prevention in Beachwood Schools

This is the policy proposal presentation I delivered to the Beachwood Board of Education on May 11, 2026. Both proposals were approved on the spot. Use it as a model for your own policy proposal presentation.

Why This Matters

  • Over 100,000 overdose deaths occur each year in the United States. (CDC)
  • Opioids are the leading cause, involving over 76% of those deaths. (CDC)
  • Overdoses can occur anywhere, including schools and public spaces.
  • In 2023, 708 adolescents died from drug overdoses, with a majority involving opioids like fentanyl. (CDC)

Naloxone Accessibility Data: 80+ pharmacies, retail stores, and public spaces across Cleveland and Boston

Naloxone is available at many pharmacies, but it is often not clearly visible and sometimes locked or restricted.

Why visibility works

  • Visibility builds recognition
  • Recognition drives action in emergencies
  • Repeated exposure increases familiarity
  • People are more likely to use what they recognize

Real examples found in field research

  • NaloxBox above AED at the Boston Museum of Science
  • Naloxone inside AED cabinet at Case Western Reserve University
  • Naloxone box beneath AED kit at Hawken Upper School

Proposal 1: Awareness

Install wall-mounted naloxone kits at the high school

  • Place in main hallways and in front of nurse's office
  • Bright, visible design
  • Clear, simple instructions or QR code

Proposal 2: Education

Integrate into health curriculum annually

  • Every student learns: overdose signs, response steps, naloxone use
  • Delivered during the substance use unit
  • Expand to middle school over time
  • This should be a system, not a one-time event

But won't this encourage more substance use?

No. Naloxone is used when someone is experiencing an overdose and cannot breathe, just like CPR is used when someone's heart has stopped. It is a life-saving response, not an encouragement of risky behavior.

What can be gained from this?

  • Lives can be saved
  • Stigma is broken, creating a culture of awareness
  • Prepares students to become capable responders
  • Beachwood can become a model district in substance use prevention.

Why This Matters: The Case for Action

National & Local Crisis

  • 80,000+ overdose deaths per year in the U.S.
  • Opioids account for over 60% of those deaths (CDC)
  • In 2023, 708 adolescents died from drug overdoses, the majority involving opioids (CDC WONDER data)

  • Cuyahoga County recorded 635 overdose deaths in 2023 (Cuyahoga County Board of Health)
  • Two-thirds of fatal overdoses have a bystander present. Naloxone is used in only 30% of those cases (AMA, 2024)

Credentials

  • Opioid research at Case Western Reserve University (NIH/NIDA-funded) for 3 years
  • Ohio Representative for the SAFE Project Youth VOICE Council & CADCA National Youth Council

Field Research: Naloxone Accessibility Data

I visited 75+ pharmacies, retail stores, and public spaces across Cleveland and Boston, collecting detailed data on price, location, signage, prescription options, brand, and visibility. Key finding: naloxone is available at pharmacies, but it is often not clearly visible, and sometimes locked or restricted. A life-saving medication is only effective if it is immediately accessible.

The Visibility Problem

  • The majority of CVS locations in Cleveland carried Narcan, but placement was inconsistent and often hidden; some pharmacy technicians could not locate it without my help
  • Price ranged from $44.99 to $130 depending on brand and location
  • Boston Museum of Science: NaloxBox mounted above an AED kit
  • Case Western Reserve University: naloxone inside an AED cabinet next to a frequently used elevator
  • Hawken Upper School: naloxone box located beneath AED kit

Our Two Proposals

Proposal 1: Awareness — Wall-Mounted Naloxone Kits

Install wall-mounted naloxone kits at Beachwood High School in high-visibility locations.

Placement:

  • Main hallways where students and staff pass daily
  • In front of the nurse's office
  • Adjacent to existing AED/emergency kits

Supporting Evidence:

  • AMA adopted formal policy: AED-adjacent naloxone in all public locations (June 2024)
  • HHS & GSA: joint federal recommendation for AED + naloxone co-location (Dec. 2023)
  • Dublin City Schools (2016) and Akron Public Schools (2017): Ohio precedent established
  • Free naloxone available through Ohio's Project DAWN

Proposal 2: Education — Annual Health Classes Module

Integrate a naloxone and opioid awareness module into the existing substance use unit of BHS Health classes, delivered annually to all students.

Every student learns:

  • How to recognize the signs of an opioid overdose
  • How to respond: call 911, rescue breathing, recovery position
  • How to administer Narcan nasal spray step by step

Evidence:

  • 8.58× greater odds of overdose recovery with bystander naloxone training (NIH meta-analysis)
  • 35 states have authorized educational settings to carry and administer naloxone

Legal Framework

Ohio HB 57 — Effective January 19, 2026

Authorizes Ohio school districts to obtain, store, and administer overdose-reversal medication. Requires a written policy covering storage, staff training, and emergency response.

AMA Policy (June 2024)

The American Medical Association formally adopted a policy supporting AED-adjacent naloxone in all public locations and called on schools specifically to increase access and education.

Consider installation of wall-mounted naloxone kits in main hallways and adjacent to AED/emergency units at BHS, consistent with Ohio HB 57 protocols.

2

Adopt an annual naloxone and opioid awareness module in the Health curriculum's substance use unit, beginning 2026-2027, with expansion to middle school.

We are prepared to assist with implementation at every step, including curriculum design, sourcing naloxone boxes at no cost through SAFE Project, and communication. Beachwood can become a model district in substance use prevention.
